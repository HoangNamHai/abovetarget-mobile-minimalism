# **Stage 1 : No paywall, no money**

Start with the hard part.

A lot of apps ship with no paywall. Free (or with ads). Or a "buy me a coffee" link. Or the classic : "I will monetize later, once I have users."

And they make nothing.

The big apps decided to charge before they had much to charge for.

- ShroomID does $70k a month on one feature, photo a mushroom, get an ID. Not because the feature is rare. Because they put a price on it.  
- Prayer Lock, one founder, a prayer app, $70k a month, with a hard paywall on day 1

None of these are ad-supported, praying for a billion installs. They ask for money directly, and early.

If your app has no paywall :

You do not have a conversion problem or a pricing problem yet. 

You have a hobby. Maybe a great one. But the money starts the day you decide to ask for it.  
Maybe for your app : ADD IT NOW

the pricing can be tweaked later no one cares.

# **Stage 2 : Now put it in the onboarding, at the front**

So now you have a paywall. Most people then hide it.

Free app, and a little "Go Pro" button buried in settings that nobody ever taps.

The user never meets the price at the moment they actually care.

The top apps do the opposite. The paywall is part of the onboarding. You meet the price on day 0, in the first few minutes, before you ever reach the home screen.

- Duolingo : 38 screens, then the paywall.  
- Cal AI : 32 screens, then the paywall.  
- Prayer Lock : 36 screens, then a hard paywall with no exit.  
- check for yourself top apps 

Why so early ?

The highest-intent minute in an app's whole life is the one right after install. 

Most trial starts happen on day 0

1. Build the loss they have not using the app across the onboarding.
2. Show the price at the end of it.

In the flow,up front, right after the user has built something they would hate to lose.

Maybe for your app :

open your own onboarding and find where the paywall sits.

If the honest answer is "it does not, it lives in settings"

 move that paywall inside the onboaridng, not at the settings of your app.

# **How do I apply this ?**

- Stage 1 : no paywall, no money. Decide to charge.  
- Stage 2 : put the paywall inside the onboarding, up front, after the user has something to lose.  
- Stage 3 : Engineered psychology tricks

Most apps  A/B test button colors on a paywall buried in settings that almost nobody reaches. That is polishing a screen no one sees.

**5 tricks every top mobile apps uses to make $10k/mo so easy to reach.**  
**They didn't get lucky. They engineered getting revenue.**

Everyone thinks the big apps got lucky.

I went through every onboarding screen slowly and just watched what they do.

To be honest : it is engineered, screen by screen.

I tore down four apps screen by screen. Duolingo. Yazio. Finch. ShroomID... (I share more daily)

I am not going to tell you what you are doing wrong. I do not know your app, and I shipped plenty of my own mistakes anyway.

Five things I noticed. Take what fits.

# **Principle 1: The sale is closed before the price ever shows up**

Here is the first thing I noticed in Duolingo.

* They make you wait 38 screens for the paywall.  
* They personalize your plan.  
* They make you finish a real lesson, so you get a win.  
* You start a streak.  
* The owl knows yuo (duo).

The price shows up dead last, at the exact moment when leaving means dumping your streak, your plan, and a bird you somehow already care about.

So the paywall is not really doing the selling. The 38 screens before it are. By the time the price appears, the user has too much to lose to walk away.

Maybe for your app:

1. open your onboarding and find the screen your paywall sits on.  
2. Look at what the user has actually built before that point. A win, a streak, a saved result, a plan.

If the honest answer is not much, it might be worth handing them one real win earlier, then placing the price right after it.

I think that single move is worth trying before you touch anything else.

# **Principle 2: Ask for the review at the peak, not after**

What I noticed in Yazio is the timing of the review ask. They do not wait for day seven.

They ask right in the middle of onboarding, when the plan feels built for you and you have not seen a price yet. You are at peak optimism.

\= So you have more chances to give that five stars.

And that matters more than it looks. Every five star review lifts their store rating, and that rating quietly converts the next stranger who finds them.

The review ask is free distribution, placed at the one moment it pays the most.

Maybe for your app:

it could be worth finding the highest-feeling moment in your flow, probably right after the plan reveal or the first win, and asking for the review there, before any price or friction.

A lot of apps ask one tap too late. Moving it earlier might be worth a test.

# **Principle 3: A "no" is the start of a second funnel, not the end**

Here is something I almost missed in Yazio:

When you decline the paywall, it does not just let you go.

* Tap close and a wheel appears.  
* You spin it.  
* You win 83% off.  
* A 24 hour clock starts.

You did not get a discount, you won a prize, and it is about to expire. Same price as the first screen, completely different feeling.

There is a whole second funnel waiting behind the close button.

Maybe for your app:

it might be worth building one thing that fires when someone dismisses your paywall:

A better offer; a longer trial; a small game; longer deadlines...

so it feels like now and not someday. When a deal feels earned rather than handed out, it tends convert more.

worth looking into.

# **Principle 4: Make them responsible for something that is not them**

What I noticed in Finch is that the streak is not about you. It’s about the mascot.

Before screen five you hatch a bird. You name it. You pick its personality.

Then they flip the whole thing. You take care of yourself, and the bird gets energy and goes on an adventure. You skip, and the bird runs low. You are not failing a counter. You are letting down something that depends on you.

That reframe is the product: People struggle to show up for themselves. They show up much more reliably for something that needs them.

Maybe for your app:

it could be worth asking whether there is something in your product the user can feel responsible for:

* friends / family  
* a pet, a plant,  
* a future version of yourself?

Tying the habit to that thing's wellbeing instead of the user's own score might be worth experimenting with. "Your garden is wilting" tends to pull harder than "you broke your streak."

# **Principle 5: Name the exact bad thing you prevent**

ShroomID is the one that surprised me most. One feature, pic that mushroom and it shows you what it is...

$70K a month

Screen one of the onboarding is a disclaimer. You tap "I agree" that eating wild mushrooms can be dangerous.

It looks like a legal formality. Watching it, I think it is the best salesperson in the app. The stakes land instantly. Wrong answer, hospital.

At the paywall they make you ask : "is $5 reasonable to not poison myself.", YES obviously

Simple: People do not pay for features. They pay to avoid a specific bad outcome, buy hope.. you name it.

Maybe for your app:

it might help to write one sentence for what goes wrong if your app fails your user.

Then it could be worth surfacing that early.

EVEN STRONGER : letting the user name it themselves

# **How can I apply this?**

None of these founders got lucky.

They figured out that the paywall is the last screen that matters, and they put their energy into the screens before it.

I spent a long time doing the opposite, polishing the paywall and arguing about button colors, and the price was never the problem.

I am not going to hand you a to-do list. You know your app better than I do. But if any of this rings true, here are five questions you could sit with this week:

1. Does the user build something they would hate to lose before they see the price?  
2. Is the review asked at the peak, before any friction?  
3. Does a "no" lead anywhere, or just into silence?  
4. Is the habit tied to something that needs them, or only their own discipline?  
5. Have you named the bad thing you prevent, or just listed features?
